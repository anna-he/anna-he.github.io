Class 10

Allyhood

I like talking about this one. Of course, since my group facilitated it!

We opened with ten minutes of self reflection with each person jotting down their personal thoughts and definitions. We then brought it back to the entire group for them to share. Our open discussion thoughts included questions like ‘what does it mean to be an ally and how does that differ from being a bystander.

I think we did a good job with the individual self-reflection. When our group met to plan this event, we all agreed that there has been a lot of great discussions, but less self-reflection for ourselves. Our intention with the beginning of the facilitation was to make sure everyone had a chance to think about themselves without feeling the need to have to share their thoughts.

Johnson: Getting off the hook: Denial and Resistance

I understand the frustration and I agree that everywhere there is so much denial and resistance to change. Especially change. The reason people do nothing is because even if they don’t deny it, they don’t feel compelled to change themselves and others

And to be honest, to not be silent and to put yourself out there for change is a challenging task! An Open Letter to Non-Black POC

Quote “As non-Black people of color, we are granted the ability to assimilate and reproduce whiteness.”

I certainly feel that way when I struggle between my Chinese heritage and my American/Canadian upbringing that it’s either white or bust. There are often jokes made about how buying starbucks is typical “White girl” move… is that what it means to reproduce whiteness? And if so, why is it that this article claims that black people cannot reproduce whiteness?

The letter says we need to examine Blackness the same way we examine whiteness but doesn’t that ostracise them from each other further? I acknowledge and appreciate the differences in culture between Black and White communities, but I think both share significant similarities and differences. And this letter to me seems to emphasize differences without much acknowledgement for the areas of grey in between.

I of course don’t want to endorse people-of-color-blindness because as a woman of color, I understand through experience how my feelings of ostracisation vary greatly from others. But collectivisation is unavoidable. I think it's part of human nature. That is also how stereotyping occurs. Because unless you take each individual persona separately, you will only find yourself in thinner and thinner groups. What’s more important is knowing that by grouping people together, it does not define them and that smaller differences are there if you look

Quote: “It is disingenuous for any of us to say we “sympathize as a fellow minority” as this erases differences and suggests that the University is an equal playing field for all students of color. It is not.”

Class 6

How microaggressions are like mosquito bites

Hahaha this is an amusing video. I’m surprised at the negativity in the comments.

I think there’s a misconception about microaggressions that make people angry about them. It isn’t about removing microaggressions entirely, awareness about microaggressions is more to do with being sensitive about others feelings to try and mitigate what can and can’t be done

A millennial explains: Why we melt

My take on microagressions: yes they exist. And they will always exist. And it’s impossible to erase them because there’s a lot you don’t know about people and no one knows what might microagress them.

Asking where someone is from is not a micro agression. Different questions can take different interpretations. What’s most important is realising that people may be sensitive so some topics and not push too hard for them. Similarly, being aware of microagressions and taking time to reflect on ourselves and why we may hold certian biases that may trigger others, and where these biases come from.

21 Racial Microaggressions You Hear on a Daily Basis

Some of these questions are innocent. But the context and the phrasing is not thoughtful. To ask someone whether or not they speak Spanish could be extrapolated from their education, not necessarily how they look. Especially if you know that they come from an ethnic background that does speak Spanish. There is a fine line between making assumptions and getting to know someone new.

Microaggressions in Everyday Life: Implications for Clinical Practice

Not all of these microagressions carry the message associated in the chart. Asking where someone is from is not necessarily implying that they are foreign or not American. It could just as much be about learning more about someone’s past.

Being told I’m articulate does not have to be a comparison against my race. It depends on intentions. In fact, becoming more articulate is one of my goals. Does that mean I see myself to be unintelligent for my race?

Quote: “I believe the most qualified person should get the job” I don’t think that’s wrong. There are a lot of assumptions that must be made to move from the microaggression to the message.
